00 · Orientation

How to use this manual

This is the reference for how the team operates. It does not describe what is happening this week — it describes the system that holds no matter who is on the team or what is in production. Roster, schedules, and current projects live in the tools; the rules for using them live here.

Every contributor reads this once during onboarding, then returns to it whenever they are unsure where something goes or how a process runs. It is maintained by the Lead (or a delegated ops owner) and reviewed every quarter. When the team changes how it works, the change is made here first, then announced — not the other way around.

The one rule

Every piece of work has one owner, one status, and one home (a link). If you cannot answer "who owns this, what's its status, where does it live?" then the system has a gap — and closing that gap is the work. Almost everything in this manual exists to make those three questions instantly answerable.

If you read nothing else: communication happens in Discord (§03), the truth about who-owns-what and where-it-stands lives in the tracker (§04), and files live in Drive (§05). The pipelines (§06) connect them. Everything else is detail.

Why Google Sheets/Docs and not Notion

Notion gets recommended a lot, and it is a genuinely better-designed product than a spreadsheet for this kind of work. But it does not fit a free, multi-person team, and that disqualifies it for now. Here is the honest reasoning:

  • Notion's free plan is built for one editor. On a free workspace, additional people join as guests, and guests can only view and comment — they cannot edit pages. The moment you want a second person updating statuses, you are pushed to the paid Plus plan (~$10 per editor / month).
  • Multi-member free workspaces hit a collaborative-block limit and a 5 MB per-file upload cap. Neither problem exists with Google Docs/Sheets.
  • Google Docs & Sheets allow unlimited editors for free, forever. Everyone already has a Google account, the learning curve is near zero, and a Sheet with dropdowns and colour rules gives you a perfectly good board (§04).
Decision

Start free with Google Sheets + Docs + Drive and Discord. Revisit Notion only if the team later has a budget — at ~$10/editor/month, Notion Plus becomes the nicer hub and a migration is worth planning then (a skeleton structure is in §04 so the option stays open).

02 · People

Roles & responsibilities

Roles are defined by function, not by person. Someone can hold more than one role, and roles can be reassigned — but every role below always has exactly one accountable owner at a time. This is what kills the "I thought you were handling it" failure.

Single-owner principle

Every task and every content piece names one owner — the person accountable for it moving forward. Others can help, but the owner is who you ask for status and who is responsible if it stalls. "Shared ownership" means no ownership.

RoleOwnsAccountable forHands off to
Lead Direction, priorities, final sign-off, the system itself. Deciding what gets made and in what order; unblocking people; keeping this manual current. Delegates execution to every other role.
Outreach & Marketing Audience growth, distribution, and guest acquisition. Finding & booking guests; promoting published content; managing social posting and community. Booked guest → Interview Host. Published video → repurposing.
Core Developer Day-to-day build of the product/site. Shipping features and fixes; keeping the codebase healthy; routine reviews. Escalates architecture / hard problems to Advisory Dev.
Advisory Developer on request High-leverage technical judgement. Architecture decisions, hard bugs, code review of risky changes, mentoring — consulted, not on the daily rota. Returns decisions/reviews to Core Dev.
Design & Short-Form Media Visual identity and short-form output. Thumbnails, graphics, brand assets, reels and shorts (cut & published). Final thumbnail/graphics → publishing. Brand kit → everyone.
Long-Form Video Editor The flagship long-form edit. Turning interview/long footage into a finished, on-brand video to spec; delivering review cuts. Review cut → Lead. Approved cut → packaging/publishing.

Adding capacity

When a role is overloaded, don't just pile work on a person — split the function into a new role and define it in this table. The Advisory pattern (a skilled specialist consulted on request rather than rostered daily) is the template for any "available sometimes" contributor: give them a clear trigger for when they're pulled in, and don't make the pipeline depend on their daily presence.

03 · Communication

The Discord staff server

The staff server is the internal workspace — separate from any public/audience community. It is where the team and trusted contributors talk, coordinate, and hold voice calls. Keeping it lean matters more than making it impressive: a server with twenty dead channels feels abandoned. Start small, add channels only when an existing one gets noisy.

Public vs staff

Never run internal work inside a public community server. The staff server is invite-only and holds planning, drafts, and anything sensitive. A public/audience server (if one exists) is a separate place entirely. Mixing them leaks plans and confuses both audiences.

Role hierarchy & permission philosophy

Roles do two jobs: they grant access and they signal who someone is. Keep the set small (a server this size needs well under a dozen roles) and set permissions at the role level, using per-channel overrides only for the few private areas. The golden rule of Discord permissions: a deny always beats an allow, so build "private by default, open by exception."

RoleTierCan do / see
OwnerAdminEverything. One person. Holds the server.
AdminAdminManage channels, roles, bots, members. One or two trusted people maximum.
CoreStaffFull member of the team. Sees all working categories incl. planning. Cannot manage the server.
Dev / Marketing / Media / EditorialAccessDepartment tags that grant visibility to that department's channels and let you @mention a whole function.
ContributorAccessScoped access — sees only the specific channels relevant to their task. Cannot see admin, planning, or other departments' private channels.
BotSystemPermissions a bot needs to function — nothing broader. Re-check after every bot re-authorisation.

Channel & category map

A lean starting structure. Red = private category (hidden from @everyone; visible only to listed roles). Everything else is visible to all staff.

📌 START HERE # visible to everyone who joins #welcome-and-rules #announcements # read-only; Core/Admin post #role-and-access # how access works; ping an Admin 🗣️ GENERAL #general #random #standup # daily async updates (see §08) 🔊 War Room # voice + screen-share for syncs/pairing 🎬 PRODUCTION #ideas-and-backlog #scripts-and-prep #footage-and-links # drop Drive / review links here #review-room # cuts go here for timestamped notes #publishing 📈 OUTREACH & MARKETING #guest-pipeline #promo-and-social 💻 DEV [private — Core, Dev, Advisory, Admin] #dev-general #builds-and-deploys 🔒 CORE [private — Core + Admin only] #planning #sensitive # anything not for contributors

Use threads liberally inside busy channels (one thread per video, per bug, per guest) so conversations stay findable instead of scrolling away. If #footage-and-links or #guest-pipeline ever gets crowded, convert it to a forum channel — but only past the point where threads in a normal channel stop coping. Below a dozen or so active people, a normal channel with threads is enough.

How to make a category private (the contributor wall)

This is the mechanic that lets a contributor join the staff server and see only their channels. Do it at the category level so every channel inside inherits the setting:

  1. Create the role first (e.g. Contributor) under Server Settings → Roles. Give it no special server-wide permissions.
  2. On the category (e.g. 🔒 CORE) → Edit Category → Permissions.
  3. Select @everyone and set View Channels = ✖ (deny). The whole category disappears for everyone by default.
  4. Add the roles that should see it (e.g. Core, Admin) and set View Channels = ✔ (allow).
  5. Make sure channels inside are set to "Sync to category" so they inherit these rules. Override an individual channel only when one channel needs to differ.

Result: a new contributor with only the Contributor role sees START HERE, GENERAL, and whatever production channels you explicitly grant them — and nothing in CORE or DEV ever renders for them.

Onboarding flow

For a private invite-only server you can skip Discord's "Community" features, but turning them on gives you a Welcome Screen, rules screening (members accept rules before they can chat), and AutoMod — all worth having. The flow:

  1. Person accepts the invite and completes rules screening.
  2. An Admin assigns their role(s) — this is what unlocks the right channels.
  3. A welcome bot posts a short greeting in #welcome-and-rules pointing to 2–3 starting channels.
  4. They post a one-line intro and pick up their first task.

Recommended bots (free) — keep it to two or three

Carl-bot

Reaction/self-assign roles, welcome messages, automod, logging. The workhorse — covers most needs alone.

Sesh or Apollo

Scheduling & events inside Discord — handy for syncs, recordings, and publish dates with RSVPs.

A reminder bot

Optional. Lightweight reminders/nudges. Don't over-automate a small team — fewer bots, fewer permissions to audit.

Etiquette norms

One topic = one thread. Daily updates go in #standup, not scattered. Reserve @here/@everyone for things that genuinely need everyone now; use department/role mentions otherwise. Move any real-time discussion that runs long into the War Room voice channel and post the outcome back as a message.

04 · Coordination

The coordination hub

One Google Sheet is the brain of the operation. It does not hold the work — it indexes it. Every row is one piece of work, and its cells link out to the script, the footage, the cut, the published URL. The Sheet answers "what's happening and where does it stand" at a glance; the depth lives behind the links.

The master tracker (one Sheet, several tabs)

Make a spreadsheet feel like a board

  • Status as a dropdown. Use Data → Data validation on the Status column with a fixed list: Backlog · Scripting · Filming · Editing · In Review · Scheduled · Published · Archived. Fixed words mean filtering and sorting actually work.
  • Colour by status. Format → Conditional formatting to tint each status a colour. Now the sheet reads at a glance like a Kanban.
  • Links as links. The Brief / Footage / Cut / Final / Published columns hold hyperlinks (paste the Drive or YouTube link, label it). The sheet stays the index; nothing heavy lives inside it.
  • Filter views per person. Each owner can save a filter view ("my open items") without disturbing anyone else's view.
The linking principle

The tracker never holds documents or media — it holds links to them. One row → its brief doc → its footage folder → its review link → its published URL. This is the entire reason status is always answerable without asking anyone.

If you prefer cards over a sheet

Trello (free) gives a drag-and-drop Kanban with unlimited cards and is friendlier for people who think in cards. The trade-off: it's weaker at holding many structured fields and cross-links than a Sheet. A reasonable split is Trello for the visual board and the Sheet for the calendar/outreach/dev tabs — but running one tool is simpler than two, so default to the Sheet unless the team genuinely prefers cards.

If the team later moves to Notion (paid)

Kept here so the door stays open. In Notion, the PIPELINE becomes a single database with multiple views of the same data — a Board view grouped by Status, a Table view, a Calendar view by publish date — plus relations linking each piece to its guest, its tasks, and its assets. It is a nicer experience; it is only worth the per-seat cost once there's a budget. The Sheet structure above maps onto it one-to-one when that day comes.

05 · Storage

Files & asset management

Files live in Google Drive in a fixed folder structure with strict naming. The structure is the difference between "I'll find it in a sec" and ten minutes of digging through someone's downloads. Two hard constraints shape everything: free Drive is 15 GB per account, and raw video is enormous.

Folder structure

One top-level WhatNow folder, owned by the Lead, shared with the team. Stable top-level folders, project folders nested inside production.

WhatNow/ 00_ADMIN/ # this manual, access list, decisions log 01_BRAND/ # logo, colours, fonts, lower-thirds, intro/outro 02_TEMPLATES/ # edit-brief, checklists, thumbnail templates 03_PRODUCTION/ # one folder per project ↓ Interview - [Guest]/ 01_Prep/ # question doc, research, brief 02_Raw/ # proxies only on free tier (see below) 03_Edit/ # project files, working exports 04_Exports/ # review cuts + final master 05_Thumbnails/ 06_Captions/ # .srt / transcript 04_PUBLISHED/ # final masters of everything shipped 05_ASSETS/ # reusable b-roll, music, SFX, stock

File naming convention

Non-negotiable, because consistent names are searchable and sortable. The pattern:

Pattern

PROJECT_TYPE_descriptor_vNN_INITIALS.ext

Examples: INT-Rao_thumbnail_v03_IF.png · INT-Rao_master_v05_FINAL.mp4 · REEL_launch-hook_v02_IF.mp4

  • Dates always YYYY-MM-DD so they sort chronologically.
  • Versions are v01, v02, v03… — never overwrite a file with the same name.
  • "FINAL" is a version, used once. When it's final, it's …_v05_FINAL. There is no FINAL_FINAL, no FINAL-real, no FINAL-use-this-one. If it changes after "final," it's v06.

Where heavy raw footage lives

Watch out — the 15 GB trap

Free Google Drive is 15 GB shared across Drive, Gmail, and Photos. A single hour of interview footage can be tens of gigabytes — it will fill a free account instantly. Do not try to keep all raw footage in the cloud.

The free-tier workflow that actually works:

  • Raw footage stays local — on the recorder's machine and backed up to an external SSD. It is not cloud-stored long-term.
  • Only lightweight things go to Drive: proxy/review cuts, final exports, thumbnails, captions, brand assets. Crucially, Google Docs / Sheets / Slides don't count against the 15 GB at all — so the entire coordination and SOP layer is free forever.
  • To hand raw files between people: use a free large-transfer tool (Smash has no hard size cap with expiring links; WeTransfer free goes to ~2 GB), or drop into a shared Drive folder temporarily and clear it after the editor pulls it down.
  • After publish: archive raw to the SSD, keep the final master in 04_PUBLISHED, and free the cloud space.

Access model

Two free-Drive gotchas

1. No Shared Drives on free. "Shared Drives" (team-owned folders) need paid Google Workspace. On free accounts you share a regular My Drive folder — which means files are owned by whoever created them. 2. Ownership leaves with the person. If a contributor who owns files departs, those files can go with them. Keep important masters owned by the Lead's account, and transfer ownership before anyone offboards (see §10).

A pipeline is a sequence of stages, each with one owner and a clear definition of "done" that triggers the handoff to the next stage. When everyone knows the stages, work flows without the Lead having to push it through manually.

6.3 — Repurposing (long-form → clips)

Every long-form video is raw material for several shorts. Make this systematic, not occasional: at packaging (stage 8 above), the editor or host flags 3–5 strong moments with timestamps directly in the card. Those become short-form cards in the backlog automatically, so one interview feeds weeks of short-form output instead of being mined ad hoc.

6.4 — Dev workflow

Lightweight and standard. Tasks live in GitHub Projects (or the DEV tab). Code in a GitHub repo (free private). Flow: pick a task → branch → open a pull request → review → merge → deploy. Core Dev handles day-to-day reviews; the Advisory Dev is pulled in for architecture decisions, risky changes, or a stuck bug — consulted, not blocking. Keep dev decisions in the repo (issues/PRs), not buried in Discord.

6.5 — Outreach / guest pipeline

Tracked in the OUTREACH tab as stages: Prospect → Contacted → Booked → Recorded → Published. Each prospect gets a personalised first message and a defined follow-up cadence (e.g. follow up after 4–5 days, then once more, then park). On booking, hand off to the interview prep stage above. After publish, send the guest the link and their clips — good guests refer the next ones.

07 · Standards

SOPs, templates & checklists

These live as Google Docs in 02_TEMPLATES and get duplicated per project. They make quality repeatable and remove the need to explain the same thing twice. Copy the lists below into Docs once and reuse forever.

Interview prep checklist

  • Guest confirmed, date in CALENDAR, calendar invite sent with the join link.
  • Format, expected duration, and topic shared with the guest in advance.
  • Question doc finalised; questions ordered; a few backups ready.
  • Recording setup tested: camera, lighting, and a separate audio recording as backup.
  • Storage space confirmed on the recording device before the session.
  • Quiet location; notifications silenced; water for the guest.

Review & feedback protocol

How to give notes

Notes are timestamped (01:24 — trim this pause), collected into one consolidated list per round, with a maximum of two rounds. Approval is explicit ("approved"), not implied by silence. Free method: upload the cut unlisted to YouTube and leave timestamped notes in the linked review doc or #review-room thread. For nicer frame-accurate annotation, Filestage (free, unlimited reviewers) or Frame.io (free, 2 users) work — see §09.

Publishing checklist — YouTube long-form

  • Title finalised (clear, searchable, not clickbait-hollow).
  • Thumbnail uploaded (on-brand, readable at small size).
  • Description written with links, guest credit, and timestamps/chapters.
  • Chapters added; captions/subtitles uploaded.
  • End screen + cards set; relevant playlist added.
  • Visibility set / scheduled; pinned comment ready.
  • CALENDAR updated; clips queued for repurposing.

Publishing checklist — short-form (per platform)

  • Strong first-second hook; caption + on-screen text checked.
  • Cover frame chosen; correct aspect ratio.
  • Hashtags / sound set per platform norms.
  • Posted at a sensible time; logged in CALENDAR.
Definition of Done

A content piece is Done only when it is published, its master is archived in 04_PUBLISHED, and its card is moved to Archived. "The edit is finished" is not done. Published-and-filed is done.

08 · Rhythm

Cadence & rituals

Async-first, because contributors keep different hours and availability. The rituals below replace the Lead manually chasing everyone for updates — the system surfaces status so nobody has to ask for it.

Daily · async

Standup post

Each active contributor drops a one-liner in #standup: what I did / what I'm on / any blocker. Thirty seconds. This is what removes the need for follow-up DMs.

Weekly · 30 min

Sync call

In the War Room. Walk the PIPELINE top to bottom, clear blockers, assign the coming week. Keep it to thirty minutes — it's a working session, not a status theatre.

Weekly · solo

Board hygiene

The Lead (or tracker owner) clears stale statuses, archives shipped work, and promotes ripe ideas from IDEAS into PIPELINE.

Weekly sync agenda

1) Anything blocked? (fix or assign) · 2) Walk PIPELINE — confirm each owner + status · 3) What ships this week? · 4) Next guest / next dev task · 5) Anything to add to this manual. That's it.

10 · Runbook

Onboarding a contributor

A fixed sequence so bringing someone on takes minutes and grants exactly the right access — no more, no less.

  1. Decide the role and access level. Are they core or scoped? This determines everything below.
  2. Discord: invite → assign their role(s) → they complete rules screening and post an intro. Their role is what reveals the right channels (§03).
  3. Google: share the relevant Drive folder(s) and the tracker, at the right permission level (Editor / Comment / none) per the access table in §05.
  4. Point them here: this manual, the brand kit, and the SOPs/templates for their role.
  5. Give a first task and a check-in. A small, clear first task beats a long briefing.
Special case — external editor / freelancer

Give: the Contributor Discord role (so they see only production channels relevant to them), Editor access to their project subfolder only, View access to the brand kit, and the edit brief.
Do not give: the full Drive, tracker edit rights, the DEV or CORE channels, or admin. Scope tight; widen later if they become core.

Offboarding — do this every time

Remove their Discord role, revoke all Drive and tracker access, and transfer ownership of any files they own to the Lead's account before they leave. On free Drive, files owned by a departing person can disappear with them — this step is not optional.

11 · Upkeep

Maintaining the system

A system only stays useful if someone owns keeping it clean. Left alone, channels go stale, folders sprawl, and free-tier limits shift under you.

  • Owner: the Lead, or a delegated ops owner, holds this manual and the structures it describes.
  • Quarterly review: prune unused Discord roles/channels, archive finished projects in Drive, re-check the tool list (free tiers change), and refresh any SOP that's drifted from reality.
  • Change deliberately, not mid-flight: don't restructure the board or folders on a whim while work is in progress. Make structural changes at review points, update this manual first, then announce.
  • Version this document: when it changes meaningfully, note the date so everyone knows they're reading the current version.
The test that catches every gap

Pick any task at random and ask: who owns it, what's its status, where does it live? If all three answers are instant, the system is working. If any answer requires asking a person, you've found the next thing to fix. That single test is the whole job of operations.
